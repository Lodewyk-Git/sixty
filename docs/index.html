The study got a bit of attention on dozens of outlets. The headline: A person’s ability to be random peaks at 25 years old and declines after 60.

We initially set out to use the findings to make an age guessing game, but after analyzing the data we lost confidence it would yield good results.

For what its worth, we have guessed right 0% of the time. Not as good as we had expected 😔.

We don’t think the randomness study’s findings are reproducible because of the researchers’ decision to include “bad” responses. To us, a bad response means the participant either misunderstood the instructions, or intentionally sabotaged the experiment. That decision was the difference between “ah this is interesting” and “welp, nothing here” (or “null results” in statistics lingo).

Here are the instructions from the original study:

Click on a number between one and six as randomly as possible to produce the kind of sequence you’d get if you really rolled a die, so that if another person is shown your sequence of digits from 1 to 6, he/she should not be able to tell whether these numbers were produced by a real die or just “made up” by somebody.

Here is an example of a good and bad dice roll sequence based on their classifying algorithm.

Do you think the bad response looks “real” and satisfies the instructions? We don’t. The researchers behind the study did (and confirmed with us via email). And this disagreement has a big statistical impact.

When we add a trend line, we can see the age drop-off finding take shape.

At the bottom are the bad responses. We even took a conservative approach to defining bad; just the ones that were entirely heads or tails.

When we exclude the bad responses, the trend disappears.

Although that example was just for the coin toss, the trend line has the same shape when you look at the aggregate results.

The study was peer-reviewed, not reproduced. This is normal since it takes significantly more time and effort to reproduce versus review. This means it is highly likely that the reviewers didn’t run their own data analysis either, which makes it far easier to miss a small but important decision like the one we found.
